Lumbar Transforaminal Epidural Steroid Injection

(Fluoroscopic-Guided Targeted Injection for Nerve Root Pain Relief)

Overview

A lumbar transforaminal epidural steroid injection (TFESI) is a minimally invasive procedure used to treat lower back and leg pain resulting from irritation or compression of spinal nerve roots. Unlike traditional epidural injections, TFESI delivers medication more precisely to the affected nerve as it exits the spinal column, offering targeted relief.

This procedure is often used when symptoms are caused by herniated discs, foraminal stenosis, or nerve root inflammation.

Conditions commonly treated with TFESI include:

  • Lumbar radiculopathy (sciatica)
  • Foraminal disc herniation
  • Foraminal stenosis
  • Nerve root inflammation from workplace or auto injury

Pre-Procedural Instructions

How Should I Prepare?

  • Driver Required: We ask that you arrange for a driver to take you home after the procedure.
  • Food: A light meal is allowed 1â€“2 hours prior to the procedure. If sedation is planned, follow the sedation protocol provided.

Medications

  • Continue routine medications unless instructed otherwise.
  • Blood thinners, NSAIDs, aspirin, and supplements:
    • Inform our office if you take medications like Eliquis, Plavix, Xarelto, Coumadin, aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, or any supplements with blood-thinning effects.
    • These may need to be held for a short period prior to the procedure. Please confirm with your prescribing provider that it is safe to stop them.
    • Our team will review your list and help coordinate if necessary.

    Notify us if:

    • You are sick, on antibiotics, or have an active infection.
    • You have had recent changes to your medications.

Sedation

Most TFESI procedures are done without sedation. If you have difficulty lying flat or experience anxiety, mild sedation may be considered by your physician.

Special Instructions for Diabetic Patients

  • Steroid injections may temporarily raise blood sugar levels.
  • Contact your endocrinologist or primary care doctor prior to the procedure to discuss any necessary adjustments.
  • Monitor your blood sugar closely following the procedure.

Procedure Details

The procedure is performed in a sterile outpatient setting under real-time X-ray (fluoroscopic) guidance.

What to Expect:

Preparation:

  • You will lie face down on a fluoroscopy table.
  • The skin is cleaned, and local anesthesia is used to numb the area.

Injection Process:

  • A needle is guided into the foramen (the nerve passageway in the spine).
  • Contrast dye is used to confirm placement.
  • A corticosteroid is delivered directly next to the inflamed nerve root.

Monitoring:

  • You will be monitored throughout the procedure and observed afterward before discharge.
  • Most procedures last 15â€“30 minutes, followed by a brief recovery period on-site.

The procedure generally takes 20â€“30 minutes.

Post-Procedural Instructions

  • Pain Relief Timeline: Improvement may begin in 2â€“5 days, but can take up to 2 weeks.
  • Activity: Light activity may be resumed the next day. Avoid strenuous activity for 24â€“48 hours.
  • Soreness: Mild soreness at the injection site is common.
  • Symptom Tracking: Track your symptoms in a pain diary for 1â€“2 weeks.

If results are positive, repeat injections may be considered. Typically, no more than 3â€“4 injections per year are performed in a given area.

Post-Procedure Care

  • Rest the day of the procedure.
  • Report concerning symptoms such as fever, weakness, or increased pain.

What are the risks and side effects?

Risks are minimal and serious complications are rare. We take every precaution to ensure safety. Potential risks may include:

  • Vasovagal reaction (fainting or dizziness)
  • Infection
  • Bleeding
  • Allergic or unexpected medication reaction
  • Temporary numbness or weakness
  • New or increased pain
  • Spinal or nerve injury (rare)

What if I am pregnant?

If there is any chance you may be pregnant, please let us know. Fluoroscopy (X-ray) used during the procedure can pose risks to an unborn fetus. The procedure should be postponed until pregnancy is ruled out.
